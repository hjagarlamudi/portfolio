Projects: Solar and Hydrogen Vehicle

Course: Principles of Engineering

Duration: 3 weeks (November 2, 2016 - November 17, 2016)

Objective: Create a vehicle that can run on solar or hydrogen power.

Type of Project: Team Project

For this project, my partners and I were assigned the task of creating a vehicle capable of running on solar or hydrogen cells. To start, we were told to create a Gantt Chart. Starting off was a little bit rough, but after brainstorming, we decided to create a simple rectangular 4-wheeler design. A problem we came across while building was preventing the solar and hydrogen cells from falling off of the vehicle. We solved this problem by adding a block at the exposed edges so the cell would not slide off. Another problem we faced was trying to fit the cells and the breadboard all onto a narrow space. To fix this problem, my team created a side pocket on the vehicle to put the breadboard into. While we were building, we were also finishing our Documentation. Through this project, my communication skills were enhanced, I had first hand experience with breadboarding, and I found more effective ways to build.

Projects: Printed Circuit Board

Course: Digital Electronics

Duration: 3 weeks (October 5, 2018 - October 29, 2018)

Objective: Create a circuit on a PCB.

Type of Project: Individual Project

For this project, I was assigned the task of creating a printed circuit board from scratch. To start, we were told to select the circuit we would create.. Starting off was simple, and I decided to create an array of blinking seven segment displays flashing my robotics team number, 139A. My next task was to create a simulation, which I built using the CDS Multisim software. The next goal I had was to test if my circuit worked in practice on a breadboard. My final goal to finish this project was to print my circuit onto a copper board and etch away extra copper. Following this, I had to drill holes and solder the components of the circuit onto the printed board. Some issues I came across were on the printing process of the board. I found myself constrained on time when printing the physical board and ended up with some disconnected copper traces on my final board. While I tried to solder and drill, I found myself breaking more traces and ultimately being unable to repair them. As a result, my printed circuit board was not functional in the end, but I did gain some valuable experience. While I was working, I was also finishing my Documentation. Through this project, my breadboarding skills were enhanced, I had first hand experience with PCBs, and I found that not every project works out in the end.
